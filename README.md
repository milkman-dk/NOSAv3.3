Abstract

This paper addresses the critical need for improved diagnostic tools in brain tumor segmentation, a manual and time-consuming process subject to inter-observer variability. This work reviews existing approaches, evaluates the feasibility of artificial intelligence (AI)-driven diagnostic tools, and develops a series of increasingly sophisticated U-Net architectures for automated brain tumor segmentation.

The paper presents two primary contributions. First, NOSA v2 demonstrates proof-of-concept using a 2-dimensional U-Net trained on 3064 T1-weighted contrast-enhanced MRI images from the Jun Cheng dataset. Second, and more substantially, the anU-Net series introduces a task-specific 3-dimensional segmentation architecture trained on the BraTS 2023 GLI dataset. The latest iteration, anU-Net v3.3, incorporates five attention mechanisms (modality attention, squeeze-and-excitation, axial attention, position attention, and attention gates), pre-activation residual blocks, and an asymmetric design with anisotropic kernels tailored to MRI physics. Trained using a whole-tumor-priority sampling strategy with combined Dice and Focal loss, anU-Net v3.3 achieves a mean Dice coefficient of 88.29% and a mean IoU of 80.41% across 5-fold cross-validation on 1251 volumes, with superior boundary precision (HD95: 5.68 mm) compared to the nnU-Net baseline (8.50 mm). In practical terms, the system can automatically outline brain tumors in MRI scans with 88% accuracy in less than one second per scan, matching expert performance while being 50-100 times faster than competing methods.

Both systems feature user-friendly graphical interfaces: NOSA v2 provides 2-dimensional visualization with heatmap-based post-processing, while NOSA v3.3 offers GPU-accelerated 3-dimensional volume rendering with VTK, morphological post-processing, metrics computation, and ground truth comparison. The paper contextualizes these technical contributions through an interview with a neuroradiologist, confirming the value of AI-driven tools as augmentations to human expertise rather than replacements. This work demonstrates that task-specific architectural innovations can achieve competitive performance with state-of-the-art models, despite having limited time and computational constraints. Future work development includes attention module fine-tuning and self-configuration similar to the nnU-Net.
